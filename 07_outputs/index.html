<div class="textcontainer"> <p class="margin"> </p> <h3>Week 7: Outputs</h3> <h4>Assignment: MVP Final Project</h4> <p> My final project was to design an adaptive, smart-sensing sole that adjusted its shape based on force applied at different points along its surface, using Velostat sensors to measure pressure and MPU-6050 sensors to measure force and movement. </p> The goal for this week was to complete a minimum viable version of my final project. The steps taken were: <br> 1. CAD the bases of the insoles in Fusion and 3D print them <br> 2. Intubate the sole with copper wiring and Velostat <br> 3. Solder wires to the copper, and cover the sole with insulative material with the exception of the Velostat tape <br> 4. Cover the other side of the sole with copper wiring such that the Velostat was situated between the copper wiring. <br> 5. Connect the microcontroller and components<br> 6. Calibrate force sensors <br> 7. Cover final part in fabric and test calibration again. <br> 8. Connect parts to stepper motor drivers (as opposed to just LEDs like in demo circuit) to convert output to actuators that will eventually alter shape. <p class="margin"> </p> <div class="class\"> <img src="IMG_4368.HEIC" alt="Early foot model"> </div> <p class="caption"></p> This first part was very tedious. It involved looking at every input of the stepper motor driver and XIAO-ESP32-C3 to figure out what inputs and outputs were connected to each other, and thus which to wire to power, which to wire to ground, and which to wire to each other. After manually drawing the relationships, KiCad allowed us to initialize all of the layers and manually draw the wiring routes, at which point the need for jump resistors presented itself. <p class="margin"> </p> <div class="class\"> <img src="Screenshot 2026-07-21 at 14.35.53.png" alt="screenshot of wiring paths in KiCad"> </div> <p class="caption"></p> After that, we had to add zero-Ohm resistors to the schematics. <p class="margin"> </p> <div class="class\"> <img src="PCB A1.png" alt="screenshot of wiring paths in monofab software"> </div> <p class="caption"></p> <p class="margin"> </p> <div class="class\"> <img src="PCB A2.png" alt="screenshot of drill points in monofab software"> </div> <p class="caption"></p> Milling the component was fairly straightforward after following the tutorial. After milling one side, we realized that the holes drilled were too thin for the components to pass through, so we needed to redo the milling with a coarser drill bit. After that, we manually mirrored the schematics in KiCad so we could mill the other side, before figuring out that it was easier to just sand the copper off the other side. After doing that, we were ready to solder. We ended up finding that there weren't enough components, so we had to learn to desolder using suction to retrieve components from older boards and use them for our own. The soldering process was fairly straightforward. <p class="margin"> </p> <div class="class\"> <img src="WhatsApp Image 2026-07-21 at 14.14.54.jpeg" alt="screenshot of PCB"> </div> <p class="caption"></p> <p class="caption">Image of the completed PCB</p> </div>
